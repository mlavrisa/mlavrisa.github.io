Fourth Year Engineering Design Project

For my fourth year design capstone project at the University of Waterloo, my team and I developed a pioneering colour-changing reflective technology. Our project won first place in our year, and focused on designing an array of plasmonic tiles immersed in a layer of liquid crystal that alter their colour with applied voltage because of the changing orientation of the liquid crystal under that voltage. We began by simulating the optical response under different refractive index conditions (n = 1.5/1.7) using the RSoft photonic simulation package, which allowed us to select the tile geometries predicted to yield the most pronounced colour shifts. The selected designs were then fabricated in Waterloo's state–of–the–art cleanroom in the QNC, and then tested—the switching speed achieved was 302 milliseconds, and the voltage–colour characteristics exhibited low hysteresis despite some non–linear behavior.

From top to bottom, it shows an ITO glass substrate, followed by a layer of liquid crystal with an interposed gasket, and finally plasmonic nanotiles deposited on an aluminum-coated silicon substrate. This detailed illustration helps in understanding the multilayer structure of the technology.

Fabrication Process: The fabrication process is depicted here. The image outlines the sequential steps: exposing the photoresist over an aluminum layer using ebeam, developing the pattern, depositing a titanium adhesion layer, adding a gold layer, and finishing with a liftoff. These steps were crucial in forming the precise features of the plasmonic tiles.

SEM Imaging: An SEM image captures the circular features produced during fabrication. This micrograph confirms the quality and uniformity of the lithographic patterning that forms the plasmonic tiles.

Simulation Results: This visualization shows the outcome of scanning the plasmonic tile width (vertical axis) versus the tile pitch (horizontal axis). The simulation estimates the resulting colour at the ordinary and extraordinary axes of the liquid crystal, guiding the selection of the most effective tile geometries.

Optimized Grid: Based on the simulation data, the pitches and widths that offered the most dramatic colour change were selected and fabricated into a 3x4 grid of patches. This grid represents the culmination of our design and optimization efforts.

Dynamic Colour Change: In this video, captured via microscope, one of the patches is shown undergoing a voltage scan. The device transitions from a greenish hue at low voltage to a light brown tone, effectively demonstrating its responsive optical behaviour.
